A modest argument against the rebrand.

Anurag Gautam & Tina Gidwani·Apr 2026·11 min read

The rebrand arrives with a particular kind of urgency. The pitch deck is ready, the new CEO is impatient, and somewhere in the brief is a sentence that reads: "We want to feel more premium." What nobody says in the first meeting — and what we have learned, slowly, to ask in the second — is what happened to make the current brand feel wrong.

Most of the time, the answer is not “the logo is bad.” It's something more specific: the company got bigger, or pivoted, or hired someone who keeps saying the word “contemporary,” or lost the original designer who knew why every decision was made. The brand didn't fail. It just stopped being explained.

We have a rule we apply before we agree to any rebrand: we ask the client to give us a tour of the current identity. Not a critique — a tour. What does this colour mean? Why this typeface and not another? What did you want people to feel when they first saw this? Usually within twenty minutes, a rebranding project becomes a recalibration project. The work is still there. The logic is still there. What was missing was someone to translate it.

“A new mark on a confused company is still a confused company. The brief comes to us because the team can't agree on what they look like.”

This is the uncomfortable thing about rebrands: they are, more often than not, a symptom of something organisational. The brief comes to us because the team can't agree on what they look like, and they've decided — reasonably, if optimistically — that a new visual identity will settle the argument. It won't. Or not for long.

The four signals we listen for

One: Nobody can explain why the current brand looks the way it does. Not the founder, not the head of marketing, not the designer who updated it last February. It exists, and everyone uses it, and no one knows what it means.

Two: The brand has multiple unofficial versions in the wild and nobody has noticed. The investor deck uses a darker version of the navy. The website uses the old wordmark. The swag vendor was given a JPEG in 2021 and ran with it. The system has fractured, slowly, and the fractures have become normal.

Three: The most recent update was made by an intern during a quiet summer, and it somehow stuck. Nobody approved it. Nobody rejected it. It became the version.

Four: The client says “I'd know it when I see it” when asked what they want. This is not a bad answer, exactly — it means the instinct is there, and instincts can be worked with. But it usually means the client has a feeling, not a brief, and the feeling is “better.”

What we offer instead

These are not signs that the brand has failed. They're signs that the organisation lost track of what it was building, and the brand just got there first.

What we offer in those cases is not a rebrand. It's closer to a translation. We read the existing system — even the inconsistent, apologetic, out-of-context version of it — and we find the idea that was always in there, half-expressed. We write it down. We extend it, carefully. And when the work is done, it usually doesn't look like a revolution. It looks like the thing it always should have been.

The clients who insist on the revolution are not wrong to want it. Sometimes the existing brand is genuinely broken, or tied to something the company no longer is, or simply so anonymous that continuing to use it is a kind of institutional shrug. In those cases, we start from zero and we enjoy it. But we ask the questions first. The tour is always worth taking.
